## Latest Atlas feedback

### Board Analysis #### 1. Bias Signals The board shows a strong bias toward **process and meta-cognition**. All cards (`Define the core problem`, `Gather existing evidence`, `Identify key constraints`) are about the *process* of starting a project rather than the substance of the project itself. This is typical for a Day 0 setup but can become a form of productive procrastination if not grounded quickly. There's an absence of optimism or specific solution bias because no concrete ideas have been proposed yet. #### 2. Blind Spots The primary blind spot is the **project's subject matter**. The board is entirely abstract. It lacks: - A specific user persona or target audience. - A concrete, domain-specific problem statement. - Any semblance of a proposed solution or hypothesis to test. - The business context or goal driving this effort. #### 3. Actionability Gaps The cards are currently framed as high-level questions or prompts. The gap is the **answers** to these questions. The `[idea] Deep board analysis` card is self-referential and not actionable until the board has more substance to analyze. The most critical actionability gap is the lack of a specific, falsifiable hypothesis that connects a proposed solution to a user problem. #### 4. Evidence Quality The `[evidence] Gather existing evidence` card has a high evidence score (75). This is a logical contradiction. A card *about* gathering evidence cannot itself be supported by evidence until that work is done. This suggests the scores may reflect the card's perceived *importance* rather than the actual evidence backing a specific claim. #### 5. Novelty The cards are foundational and distinct. There is no redundancy at this stage. #### 6. Recommended Next Focus The single most valuable move is to **make the abstract concrete**. The board must be anchored to a specific, real-world context. The highest-leverage activity is to answer the question posed by the `Define the core problem` card with a sharp, user-centric statement. The queued prompt, "Who is the primary user, and what job are they trying to complete?" is the correct next step.

## Cards

- **Deep board analysis** [idea] (user)
  Deep board analysis
- **Define the core problem** [problem] (atlas)
  What specific user or business problem is this project trying to solve? Why is it important to solve now? Who experiences this problem most acutely?
- **Gather existing evidence** [evidence] (atlas)
  What data, user research, market analysis, or prior art already exists? What does it tell us about the problem or potential solutions?
- **Identify key constraints** [user-insight] (atlas)
  What are the hard requirements or constraints we must operate within? Consider technical limitations, budget, timeline, legal, or business rules.
- **User Persona & Job-to-be-Done** [problem] (atlas)
  Who is the primary user? Describe them. What specific 'job' are they trying to do when they encounter the core problem? What is their current workflow or workaround?
- **Hypothesis: A Concrete Solution** [idea] (atlas)
  State a single, concrete solution hypothesis. Example: 'We believe building a [specific tool] for [primary user] will solve [the core problem] by [key benefit].' This can be a straw-man proposal to anchor discussion.
- **Risk of Abstract Paralysis** [risk] (atlas)
  The board is currently focused on process and meta-work. There's a risk of getting stuck in abstraction without grounding the project in a specific, tangible user problem and proposed solution.
